Theoretical Foundation

The Brain as a
Predictive Dreamer

Modern neuroscience reveals the brain not as a passive mirror of reality, but as an active simulation engine — continuously generating predictions about the world and correcting errors when they arise. This framework, most rigorously formalized in Karl Friston's Free Energy Principle, holds that every act of perception, every emotion, every social judgment is the brain's attempt to minimize prediction error.

When early cinema audiences fled theaters from the sight of an approaching train — only a projection — they illustrated this principle vividly. Their predictive models screamed danger, overriding all abstract knowledge. The model dominated the reality.

Crucially, this predictive machinery does not stop at the physical world. We build elaborate internal simulations of other minds — modeling the beliefs, intentions, and emotional states of those around us. This is the capacity known as Theory of Mind.

The Core Framework

Four Mirrors of the Self

Your Mind

Selbstbild  —  Who am I?

"I am reliable."

Fremdbild  —  How I see you

"She is sensitive to abandonment."

Selbstbild II  —  How you see me

"She thinks I am unreliable."

Fremdbild II  —  How you see yourself

"She sees herself as independent."

Gap

Her Mind

Selbstbild  —  Who am I?

"I feel insecure in this relationship."

Fremdbild  —  How I see you

"He is unreliable."

Selbstbild II  —  How you see me

"He thinks I am too sensitive."

Fremdbild II  —  How you see yourself

"He sees himself as reliable."

Each person holds four internal models simultaneously. Conflict emerges from the gaps between corresponding layers — not from reality itself, but from mismatched simulations of it.

Where Conflict Lives

The Gap at the Heart
of Every Argument

Conflict does not arise from disagreement about reality. It arises from mismatches between internal models. The most destabilizing gap is between your Selbstbild ("I am reliable") and your Selbstbild II ("she sees me as unreliable") — a discrepancy that strikes at identity itself, triggering defensiveness and emotional disconnection.

A second gap can simultaneously exist between your Fremdbild (your model of the other person) and your Fremdbild II (how you think they see themselves). You believe she is reacting emotionally; she believes she is being perfectly rational. Both experiences are internally coherent. Neither is simply wrong.

"You are not arguing about what happened.
You are arguing about whose model of whose mind
is closest to the truth."

Resolution Strategies

Three Paths Through the Fog

Each approach targets a different model layer — most fail because they target the wrong one.

Approach 01

Direct Convincing

Supply evidence to correct her Fremdbild of you. Point to consistent past behavior. Present data.

"But I have shown up consistently. Look at the past months."

⚠ Often fails — you are working from your Selbstbild II, which may itself be inaccurate. You may be correcting the wrong layer entirely.

Approach 02

Meta-Level Probing

Ask questions that refine your Selbstbild II — your model of how she sees you. Find where the gap truly lies.

"When you say you can't trust me — what do you think I believe about commitment?"

✓ More powerful — reveals whether the conflict is about reliability, emotional availability, or something else entirely.

Approach 03

Exploring Her Self-Model

Probe your Fremdbild II — how you imagine she sees herself. Discover hidden contradictions in her self-conception.

"When you think about yourself in relationships, how do you see your own needs?"

✓ Breakthrough potential — when her answer updates your model, both minds can finally work on the same problem.

Horizons of the Theory

What the Model
Cannot Yet See

Every model casts shadows. Acknowledging the limits of this framework is part of its intellectual honesty.

🎭

Strategic Deception

People deliberately engineer others' Fremdbild through omission, framing, and performance. The framework does not yet account for intentionally distorted models.

⚡

Layer-Specific Emotions

Gaps in Selbstbild vs. Selbstbild II provoke identity threat; gaps in Fremdbild vs. Fremdbild II provoke disappointment. These require different resolutions.

🔄

Update Dynamics

How do the four submodels cascade when one is suddenly revised? Which layers are most resistant to change? These remain open empirical questions.

🌐

Perfect Alignment

Due to communication and modelling limits, perfect synchronization of internal models is impossible. Resolution means reducing the gap, not eliminating it.

Looking Forward

Where the Model
May Illuminate

The framework's most immediate application is clinical: conflict therapists already work intuitively with these dynamics, but lack a shared structural vocabulary. Naming the specific model layer where conflict lives could allow for more precise, less emotionally reactive interventions.

For individuals with Autistic Spectrum Disorder, the explicit architecture may offer something that social skills training typically cannot — not a set of rules to memorize, but a causal map explaining why conflicts arise and where to look when they do. ASD often makes the automatic, intuitive updating of social models effortful or opaque; making this architecture visible may offer meaningful relief.

Finally, deception deserves treatment as a first-class subject. A deliberate lie is not merely communication — it is an act of model engineering, seeding inaccuracies into layers the recipient has no reason to distrust. When discovered, the sudden, involuntary revision of multiple submodels at once may produce the most destabilizing conflicts the framework can describe.
