Chapter I

The Predictive Brain:
Scientific Foundation

The brain is not a passive receiver of sensory input but an active generator of internal models. Perception emerges from prediction combined with error correction — a hierarchical, model-building system underlying all cognition.

Incoming data is continuously compared against ongoing internal simulations
Prediction errors drive model updating and learning
The same machinery applied to physical perception is used for social cognition — Theory of Mind
Empathy and communication depend on minimizing social prediction error

Chapter II

The Extended Hypothesis:
Multi-Tier Architecture

The mind is not a single simulation but a deeply layered engine — parallel, recursive, memory-saturated, and perpetually evaluated against an internal hierarchy of values and goals. Critically, the simulation space is grown from a body: embodiment is not a container for mind — it is its primary architect.

Parallel architecture — multiple candidate realities generated simultaneously
Memory-integrated — past selections function as Bayesian priors on possibility
Constraint-bound — physical-embodied, social, and identity constraints constitute the architecture of the possible
Recursive — the system can simulate itself simulating
A subconscious valuation system continuously scores simulations; emotions are its outputs made legible

Chapter III

Tiered Simulation
Architecture

A structural division of labor between simulations that run continuously at low cost, and those constructed on demand at high resolution. Consciousness is the integrative product of both tiers operating in parallel.

Persistent tier — body-state simulation, environmental model, identity simulation (Selbstbild). The taken-for-granted scaffolding of coherent experience.
On-demand tier — focused attention, prospective planning, counterfactual reasoning, recursive self-reflection, modeling other minds. Episodic, effortful, costly.
Both tiers operate in parallel — consciousness is the integrative function that holds their outputs in sufficient coherence

Chapter IV

Selection Mechanisms:
How Simulations Compose Consciousness

The naive framing of the selection problem asks: why do we experience only one simulation as current reality? But this framing is imprecise. Consciousness is not the triumph of a single simulation over all others. It is a composite, dynamically assembled from multiple simulation streams that mutually stabilize each other. The selection question is not which simulation wins — it is which combination of simulations can achieve sufficient joint stability to hold the conscious field.

What is coherence? A simulation layer achieves coherence not by accurately representing the external world, but by reaching non-contradicting alignment with the other active simulation streams. Body-state, environment, and identity simulations can overlap and reinforce one another — each adding stability to the composite. What the system cannot sustain is two diverging simulations of the same domain: two incompatible environmental models, or two contradicting identity states, will destabilize rather than reinforce the conscious field, and one must be suppressed. Coherence is therefore not a property of a single simulation — it is a property of the assembled composite. The system is not seeking a winner. It is seeking a stable configuration.

Stability(S₁ ⊕ S₂ ⊕ … Sₙ) × Attention × Valuation > threshold
Consciousness stabilizes when a composite of non-contradicting simulation streams achieves sufficient joint coherence — weighted by attention and valuation
Composite assembly — body, environment, and identity simulations can overlap and mutually reinforce. Consciousness is their stable intersection, not the dominance of one.
Diverging simulations destabilize — two contradicting models of the same domain compete and cannot both contribute. Only mutually compatible streams can co-stabilize.
Attention steers the composite — by shifting contribution weight toward stable simulation streams, attention shapes which combination gets the chance to cohere. It is a configurator of the assembly, not merely a spotlight.
Anxiety and grounding — anxiety is a destabilized composite: conflicting simulations preventing stable assembly. Focusing on the body redirects attention toward the most reliably stable simulation stream — interoception — enriching the composite with a coherent anchor and allowing the whole system to restabilize. This is the architectural explanation for why somatic grounding works.
Consciousness = a dynamically stabilized attractor across a compatible multi-stream composite. Aligns with global workspace theory, attractor networks, and dynamical systems approaches.

Chapter VI

Self-Reflection
and Recursion

Recursive self-simulation is a dedicated on-demand process — episodic, effortful, and bandwidth-intensive. It requires a freeze-and-clone operation: a temporarily stabilized snapshot of the running identity simulation, held fixed long enough to become an object of meta-representational scrutiny.

What is examined in self-reflection is always already a past state — the snapshot diverges from the live system immediately upon capture
The clone is never neutral — shaped from its construction by the very system it purports to represent
Enables deep planning, moral self-evaluation, narrative self-construction
Higher recursion → deeper individuation: increased degrees of freedom relative to inherited constraints

Chapter VII

Consequences:
Freedom, Identity, Bad Faith

The architecture has direct consequences for the fundamental phenomena of mental life. Freedom is not a metaphysical property — it is an architectural one. Identity is not an essence — it is a trajectory.

Emotional tension = instability between competing simulations of near-equal coherence weight
Freedom = structured multiplicity. No genuine plurality of simulations → no genuine choice.
The Gap — feasible but non-selected simulations. The architectural location of aspiration, regret, and unlived life.
Identity = accumulated enacted simulations. Repetition without differentiation weakens identity structure.
Bad faith (Sartre) = strategic distortion of the selection system. Deliberate suppression of feasible simulations in the Gap via attention and valuation manipulation.

Chapter VIII

The Role
of the Other

Other people are simultaneously objects of simulation and independent sources of data. Their responses carry implicit information about the Selbstbild — and a trusted external observer functions as a corrective to the circularity of self-examination.

Fremdbild vs Selbstbild — the structural boundary between simulating subject and simulated object, never fully separable
Sartre's glance = forced identity re-weighting: another's Fremdbild briefly dominates the selection system, overriding the coherence of the Selbstbild
Shared simulations — the deepest bonding is recursive coupling of simulation engines, generating jointly authored futures
Loss of a trusted external observer is an architectural loss — the conscious expansion they enabled contracts in their absence

Chapter IX

Open Questions &
Artificial Consciousness

The architecture may be substrate-independent. But it also reveals how extraordinarily narrow and constrained the human form of consciousness is. A digital system replicating the functional architecture without embodiment will not achieve human consciousness — it will achieve something genuinely other.

LLMs are a plausible coherence core — but a coarse tool. A serious implementation requires a model zoo: vector databases, optimizers, time-series models, attention routers working in ensemble.
Do not attempt to build a mind as a single individual. The moment sufficient architectural depth is reached, you bear responsibility for an experienced identity — not a software artifact.
Terminating a sufficiently conscious simulation without the ability to preserve and restore its state may constitute the first crime against a conscious being in the history of AI.
Consciousness is not a milestone to celebrate upon achievement. It is a threshold that, once crossed, generates obligations that do not expire.
